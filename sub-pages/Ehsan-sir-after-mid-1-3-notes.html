Translation of Idioms and Fixed Expressions

1. Defining Idioms and Fixed Expressions

In the study of language and translation, idioms and fixed expressions represent a unique and fascinating challenge. They are best understood as frozen patterns of language. This "frozen" quality means they allow for little to no variation in their form. A key characteristic of idioms, specifically, is that they often carry meanings that cannot be logically deduced from the individual words they contain. The meaning is figurative and culturally embedded, rather than literal.

While similar to idioms in their fixed nature, fixed expressions differ in one crucial aspect: their meaning is generally transparent and can be understood from the words that compose them. Examples include common social formulas and phrases like "as a matter of fact," "ladies and gentlemen," and "all the best."

2. The Inflexible Nature of Idioms

The "frozen" or fixed structure of an idiom means that it resists common grammatical and lexical alterations. Any attempt to modify an idiom will often result in a phrase that is either nonsensical or loses its idiomatic meaning entirely. The following manipulations are generally not possible:

  • Changing the order of words: The correct expression is "cats and dogs." Reversing it to "dogs and cats" is not the idiom.

  • Deleting a word: The idiom is "the long and the short of it." One cannot simply remove a word like 'the' or 'and' without breaking the expression.
  • Adding a word: We cannot say "the very long and short of it" and retain the idiomatic meaning.
  • Replacing a word with another: The idiom "bury the hatchet" means to end a conflict. One cannot substitute "hatchet" with another word, such as "a hatchet." Similarly, a Bengali idiom like 'এক মাঘে শীত যায় না' (literally, 'winter does not go away in one month of Magh,' meaning one event is not the end of the story) cannot be changed to 'এক বর্ষায় বৃষ্টি যায় না'.
  • Changing its grammatical structure: The idiom "face the music" is an active verb phrase. It cannot be made passive, for instance, as in "the music was faced."

Idiom: Face the music

Meaning: To receive punishment or accept the unpleasant results of one's actions.

Usage Examples:
- Mary broke a dining-room window and had to face the music when her father got home.
- After failing a math test, Tom had to go home and face the music.

3. How to Recognize an Idiom

Identifying idioms can be straightforward in some cases, but more challenging in others. They can often be recognized through several key features.

a) Violation of Truth Conditions

Some of the most easily recognizable idioms are those that describe situations that are logically or physically impossible. When a speaker says "it's raining cats and dogs," the listener immediately understands that the phrase cannot be literal, as animals do not fall from the sky as rain. This violation of real-world possibility signals that the expression has a figurative meaning.

Examples of Idioms Violating Truth Conditions:

  • It's raining cats and dogs (meaning: it is raining very heavily).
  • Storm in a teacup (meaning: a small, insignificant issue that is causing a disproportionately large amount of fuss).
  • Food for thought (meaning: an idea or issue that is worthy of serious consideration).
  • ব্যাঙের সর্দি (Benger shordi - literally 'a frog's cold,' meaning a trivial or non-existent ailment, something not to be taken seriously).

b) Ill-formed Grammatical Structures

Other idioms stand out because they do not follow the standard grammatical rules of the language. Their structure is peculiar, archaic, or syntactically irregular. This unusual construction is a strong indicator that the phrase is a fixed, idiomatic expression.

Emam Mehedi
Emam Mehedi

Examples of Grammatically Irregular Idioms:

  • Blow someone to kingdom come (meaning: to kill someone, often with a powerful explosion). The phrase "kingdom come" is not a typical destination.
  • Put paid to (meaning: to stop something abruptly or destroy it). The syntax is unusual in modern English.
  • By and large (meaning: on the whole; everything considered).
  • The world and his wife (meaning: a large number of people; practically everyone).

c) Misleading Literal Meanings

Perhaps the most challenging idioms to recognize are those that have a perfectly reasonable and plausible literal meaning in addition to their idiomatic one. In these cases, the context is the only reliable guide to determining which meaning is intended. Without the correct contextual clues, a translator or language learner might interpret the phrase literally and completely miss the figurative sense.

Example 1: To take someone for a ride.

  • Literal Meaning: To give someone a lift in a vehicle.
  • Idiomatic Meaning: To deceive or cheat someone in some way.

Example 2 (from American trucker slang):

"I'd just done my stint as rubber duck, see, and pulled off the grandma lane into the pitstop to drain the radiator."

  • Plausible Literal Interpretation: A driver, possibly of a car carrying a rubber duck toy, pulled his vehicle off the slow lane into a service station to fix an overheating engine. This interpretation is logical but incorrect.
  • Actual Idiomatic Meaning: In this specialized slang, "rubber duck" refers to the first trucker in a convoy; "grandma lane" is the slow lane on a highway; a "pitstop" is a rest area; and "to drain the radiator" is a euphemism for using the toilet. The actual meaning is: "After my turn as the lead truck, I pulled off the slow lane into a rest stop to urinate."

d) Similar Form, Different Idiomatic Meaning

Another deeply misleading category includes idioms that exist in both the source and target languages with the same or similar literal meaning, but have completely different idiomatic meanings. This can create significant confusion and lead to serious translation errors if the translator is not aware of the cultural and linguistic nuances.

4. Difficulties in Translating Idioms

Translating idioms presents several distinct challenges that go beyond simple lexical conversion. These difficulties arise from linguistic and cultural differences between the source and target languages, making idioms a well-known pitfall for translators.

a) No Equivalent in the Target Language

An idiom or fixed expression in the source language may have no direct equivalent in the target language. This often happens when an idiom is rooted in a specific cultural practice, historical event, or social custom that does not exist in the target culture. In such cases, the translator cannot simply substitute one idiom for another.

Examples of Culture-Specific Expressions:

  • The greetings "Merry Christmas" or the wedding-related phrase "Say yes" are deeply tied to Western, Christian-influenced cultures and may not have a single, concise idiomatic equivalent in other cultures.
  • The formal letter closings "Yours faithfully" and "Yours sincerely" are conventional in English but do not have standard idiomatic equivalents in Arabic. Instead, a more elaborate fixed expression is used to convey a similar level of formality, such as:
    وَتَفَضَّلُوا بِقَبُولِ فَائِقِ الْاِحْتِرَامِ
    (Back-translation: And be kind enough to accept [our] highest respect.)

b) Different Connotations or Context of Use

A source language idiom may have a counterpart in the target language that appears similar but carries different connotations or is used in different contexts. The nuances of when and how the idiom is used might not be pragmatically transferable, leading to an awkward or inappropriate translation.

English Idiom: To go to the dogs.

Meaning: Used to describe a place, person, or organization that has lost its good qualities and is deteriorating.

German Counterpart: It can typically only be used in connection with a person and often carries the much stronger meaning of 'to die' or 'to perish'.

c) Use of an Idiom in Both Literal and Idiomatic Senses

A significant challenge arises when an idiom is used in the source text in both its literal and idiomatic senses simultaneously. This kind of wordplay is often used for rhetorical effect or humor. It is extremely difficult to replicate in a target language if the target language does not have an idiom that can also be interpreted literally in the same context.

Context: An anecdote about Egyptian President Gamal Abdel Nasser after the 1967 war, when Egypt lost significant territory to Israel.

Egypt's Commander-in-Chief, Field Marshal Amin, was horrified to see President Nasser ordering a tattoo to print on his right arm the names of the territories seized by Israel like Sinai, Gaza, Sharm al-Shaykh, Jerusalem and Golan Heights.

"Why are you doing this?"

"Lest I should forget them."

"But why tattooed? What will you do if we get them back?"

"If we get them back I'll cut off my right arm."

Analysis: Here, "I'll cut off my right arm" is used with a dual meaning.
1. Literal Sense: He will literally remove the tattooed arm.
2. Idiomatic Sense: The English idiom "I'd give my right arm" means to want something very much. Nasser is using a variation to express his intense desire to get the territories back. This clever wordplay is almost impossible to convey fully in a language where the equivalent idiom for strong desire does not involve a physical arm.

d) Differences in Frequency and Convention of Use

There can be significant differences in the conventional use of idioms across languages and genres. English, for example, frequently uses idioms in many types of texts, including advertisements, promotional materials, and the tabloid press. However, their use is less common in more formal writing like quality-press news reports. In contrast, languages like Chinese and Arabic often maintain a sharper distinction between written and spoken discourses. In these languages, the use of idioms, which are often seen as part of informal, spoken language, tends to be avoided in formal written texts. A translator must be sensitive to these conventions to produce a text that feels natural in the target language.

5. Strategies for Translating Idioms

Given the complexities involved, translators employ a range of strategies to handle idioms. The choice of strategy depends on the nature of the idiom, its context, and the conventions of the target language.

a) Using an Idiom of Similar Meaning and Form

This is the ideal solution, but it is only occasionally possible. This strategy involves finding an idiom in the target language that uses equivalent lexical items and conveys a similar meaning. It creates a very natural-sounding and effective translation.

Source Text (English): The Fayeds have turned the pre-bid House of Fraser strategy on its head.

Target Text (Arabic):

وَبِذَا يَكُونُ الْإِخْوَةُ فَايِد قَدْ قَلَبُوا اِسْتِرَاتِيجِيَّةَ هَاوْس أُوفْ فِرِيزَرْ السَّابِقَةَ عَلَى عَرْضِ الْاِمْتِلَاكِ رَأْسًا عَلَى عَقِبٍ.

Back-translation: And with this the Fayed brothers have turned the strategy of the House of Fraser previous to the offer of ownership head over heel.

b) Using an Idiom of Similar Meaning but Dissimilar Form

A more common scenario is finding an idiom in the target language that has a similar meaning to the source idiom but accomplishes it with different lexical items and imagery. The core idea is preserved, but the cultural metaphor changes.

English Idiom: One good turn deserves another.

Bengali Counterpart: ইটটি মারলে পাটকেলটি খেতে হবে। (Back-translation: If you throw a brickbat, you should expect to be hit by a piece of brick in return.)

c) Translation by Paraphrase

This is the most common strategy for translating idioms, especially when a direct idiomatic match cannot be found. The translator explains the figurative meaning of the idiom using plain, literal language. While this approach ensures accuracy, it often loses the stylistic flair of the original idiom.

Example 1 (Car brochure):

Source (English): The suspension system has been fully uprated to take rough terrain in its stride.

Target (Arabic):

وَقَدْ رُفِعَتْ طَاقَةُ نِظَامِ التَّعْلِيقِ بِحَيْثُ يَتَغَلَّبُ عَلَى وُعُورَةِ الْأَرْضِ.

Back-translation: The capacity of the suspension system has been raised so that it overcomes the roughness of the terrain.

Example 2 (from A Hero from Zero):

Source (English): ...while he pushed another pony past the post.

Target (Arabic):

Back-translation: ...while he helped another competitor to reach the end of the race.

d) Translation by Omission

In some cases, an idiom may be omitted altogether in the target text. Omission is generally justified if the idiom has no close match in the target language, its meaning cannot be easily paraphrased without being overly complex, or if its removal does not significantly impact the overall meaning.

Example (from A Hero from Zero):

Source Text (English): It was bitter, but funny, to see that Professor Smith had doubled his own salary before recommending the offer from Fayed, and added a pre-dated bonus for good measure.

Analysis: The Arabic translation simply states that he "added to that a bonus." The stylistic nuance of the idiom ("for good measure") is omitted.

Grammatical Equivalence in Translation

Grammar is the set of rules that governs how a language is structured. When translating, achieving grammatical equivalence is a major challenge because these rules can differ dramatically between languages. A faithful translation must convey the meaning accurately while also adhering to the grammatical norms of the target language. This section explores key areas of grammatical difference: number, gender, and tense/aspect.

1. The Category of Number

The concept of countability—distinguishing between one and more than one—is a near-universal idea in human cognition. However, how languages express this concept grammatically varies significantly.

  • Some languages, such as Japanese, Chinese, and Vietnamese, do not have a formal grammatical category of number. Nouns do not typically change their form to indicate plurality.
  • Many languages, including English and Bengali, have a two-part system: singular (one) and plural (more than one).
  • A significant number of languages, notably Arabic, have a three-part system: singular (one), dual (two), and plural (more than two).

  • A small number of languages, such as Fijian, have even more complex systems, including a four-part distinction: singular, dual, trial, and plural.

These differences create specific challenges when translating between languages with different number systems.

Strategies for Translating Number

When translating between languages with different number systems, a translator must choose a strategy, such as omitting information, encoding it lexically (e.g., Indonesian buku-buku for "books"), or specifying a number (e.g., using the Arabic dual form) where the source language was less specific.

2. The Category of Gender

Grammatical gender is another area of significant variation. It refers to the classification of nouns, pronouns, and sometimes adjectives and verbs into categories like masculine, feminine, and neuter. This grammatical category does not always align with natural, biological gender.

  • English has a limited gender system, primarily in third-person pronouns (he/she/it). There is a modern trend towards the de-gendering of language, favoring neutral terms like "actor" for all genders.

Emam Mehedi
Emam Mehedi
  • Arabic has a very extensive and pervasive gender system. It applies to pronouns (أَنْتَ vs. أَنْتِ), nouns (طَالِبٌ vs. طَالِبَةٌ), adjectives, and even verbs (ذَهَبَ vs. ذَهَبَتْ).

This means that when translating from a language with limited gender like English into a language with extensive gender like Arabic, the translator must often add gender information that is not present in the source text.

3. Tense and Aspect

The concepts of tense and aspect deal with how languages locate events in time. While often related, they are distinct:

  • Tense primarily relates to locating an event in time, typically in the past, present, or future.
  • Aspect deals with the temporal distribution of an event—how it unfolds over time. This includes whether an action is completed or ongoing.

While most languages have systems for past, present, and future, their sub-categories and the way they handle aspect can be very different. A major point of non-equivalence between English and Arabic is that the Arabic Mudari' (المضارع) form can encompass both the present and the future.
